Overview of Statistics

This interactive map shows where crashes happened in the 2024 calendar year. Although many points are shown at default, users can filter crashes based on the type of injury sustained as a result of the crash: serious, minor, and possible injury. As a result, these different types of injuries are also represented with a different color. By filtering for only crashes leading to serious injuries, we see that a majority of these crashes happen on major highways and roads, instead of residential areas. In addition, it seems like the weather conditions do not seem to have an affect on the severity of these crashes whatsoever because some of these crashes happened in clear weather. On the map, this is represented by solid color lines like pink and yellow. Some recurring roads that have had serious injuries take place include Georgia Avenue, Frederick Road, and Eisenhower Memorial Highway. It makes sense that more serious injuries would happen on these types of roads, but it’s important to acknowledge this to further investigate what can be done on the roads to minimize the number of crashes that lead to fatal or serious injuries.

This side by side bar plot displays the number of crashes that occur in Montgomery County based on the weather type and the injury severity sustained by the driver. For ease of reading this plot, only the top four occurrences in weather type have been included. This type of plot helped us get an initial reading of how much of each categorical data type we were dealing with. For example, we see that we have many more crashes that occur in clear weather compared to any other weather type. From this plot, we also see that there are over twenty thousand crashes occurring in clear weather that lead to either a possible or minor injury. We also see that there are slightly over two hundred crashes that occur in each rain and snow; this could be due to government officials telling residents of Montgomery County to stay off the roads in severe weather like this to keep people who don’t need to be driving off the roads.

This is a stacked area chart that displays the evolution of multiple groups' values on the same graphic - visualizing the proportion of crash severities on each date during clear weather conditions over time (from early 2024 to March 2025). Each color represents a different severity level, and the vertical axis shows the proportion (%) of crashes on that specific day. This graph focuses mostly on clear weather, as it has the most occurrences of car crashes in the past year. The x-axis represents a time series of clear weather conditions from early 2024 to March 2025, while the y-axis portrays the percentage of crashes (0-100%) attributed to each severity level on a given day. The legend on the top right shows the 5 common severity levels: fatal injury (very rare), no apparent injury (dominates the graph), possible injury, suspected minor injury, and suspected serious injury (not as common as minor injury but more common than fatal). Possible injury represents data that have not yet been classified as minor or serious injuries but are most likely under one of those categories.

This heatmap depicts the proportion of accidents which resulted in major injury or fatality for different weather types (displayed on the y-axis) and vehicle age (displayed on the x-axis). This plot demonstrates a significantly greater risk for individuals driving older vehicles to sustain significant injury in the event of a crash. Perhaps counterintuitively, this graph also suggests that older cars do not see a decrease in safety relative to newer cars in more extreme weather, like snow and rain. The data suggests that, instead, the difference in major injury likelihood between newer and older cars decreases.
